_This post is a response to a number of people asking me how they can become a software developer without a degree, so in future I will point them in the direction of this post. I intend to come back and update this with any other wisdom I gather as I get further into my career. If you are one of these people, then welcome!_

# Introduction

If I were to ask somebody what qualifications I would need to get into software development, they would probably tell me I needed a degree in computer science or software engineering, and that I'd also need to be some kind of genius. I never went to university, and quite frankly I barely made it through my A-Levels thanks to a gripping addiction to World of Warcraft. I'm also not a genius by any stretch of the imagination, as my other half will often point out.

The tech industry is in the midst of a massive skills shortage, and this problem doesn't look like it's going away any time soon. Despite the number of people studying computer science going up every year, a lot of degrees are still failing to teach people the right skills to succeed in the industry after graduation. There's the [infamous blog post](https://blog.codinghorror.com/why-cant-programmers-program/) from Jeff Atwood that is still every bit as true despite being thirteen years old. Companies are desperate to hire developers, so they are looking at other avenues such as bootcamps or apprenticeships to build their workforce, and of course those of us that are self taught.

# Learning syntax is not learning to code

I've helped a number of people learn how to code, and something I see very often is that they will try to memorise the syntax of a language before they even try to write anything. Programming is more about solving problems than writing code, and the best way to get better at solving problems is to actually solve problems.

If you forget the syntax, or are unsure of a method name, __google it__. It's one of the worst kept secrets in the industry that even the best developers are googling on a daily basis. To solve a problem you must break it down into smaller problems, and from there you write code to solve those smaller problems. Google can tell you how to add an item to a list in Python, but it cannot tell you how your business logic should work.

# Don't just follow a tutorial

Websites like [Code Academy](https://codecademy.com) are absolutely great for getting you started with the very basics of coding, but there comes a point where you really need to build something of your own to progress. Anybody can follow a tutorial, but real growth happens when you start your own project, break it, and then figure out how you fix it. 

You're better off coming up with your own project, as you'd understand the problem better, but if you're really stuck for ideas then you could try the classic __to-do list__. Another suggestion would be to finish a tutorial project and then try adding additional features to it, such as adding __review__ functionality to your Movie List site.

# There's no shame in marketing

When it comes to applying for a job, you'll be up against computer science graduates, so you need to make sure your application stands out from the crowd. By this, I don't mean using a fancy font on your CV, or writing it in pig latin, but instead having a portfolio of work to show and conveying that you are a serious candidate. There's an industry stereotype that developers hate marketing themselves, so this means you don't have much to contend with. Some ideas for marketing yourself include:

* __Put all of your code on Github:__ It doesn't matter how simple or boring your code is, get it on there. Employers just want to see that you actually know how to code, and having a few active repositories on your Github will do absolute wonders for your prospects.
* __Attend local meetups:__ There is a common misconception that you need to be already working as a developer to attend a meetup, but this couldn't be further from the truth. Most meetups are incredibly welcoming, and these meetups are a great way to get talking to other developers. You never know, one of these connections could land you your first job!
* __Start blogging:__ Keeping a blog talking about the things you've been learning or building is great for a number of reasons. Not only does it show you are passionate about the industry, but also helps to solidify your knowledge by trying to explain it to other people. A blog can be hosted completely free by using [Github Pages](https://pages.github.com/), which will even give you a funky `.github.io` url! If you don't want to commit to a full scale blog yet, start by posting on Twitter with the relevant hashtags to promote interaction.

# Find a mentor

This is something that I cannot recommend enough. Having someone that is already working in the industry to give you advice is something that made my journey of learning to code significantly easier. This sounds like it wouldn't be an option for a lot of people, but the reality is there are a lot of experienced developers that are more than happy to pass on wisdom to the next generation. This mentor can be there to help with coding questions, career advice and even interview prep if you get as lucky as I did.

This isn't just until you get into the industry. To this day I still maintain relationships with several mentors who are there to field questions I have about my career, and give me advice when I need it.

# Get involved online

The developer community practically lives on the internet, so why not get involved? Twitter, Reddit and LinkedIn are all great online spaces for developers to communicate and share ideas (and sometimes argue!), so as an aspiring developer you should absolutely get involved! Social media for developers is something I plan to write a dedicated post about in future, as there is much to be said on this topic.

I would just like to take this opportunity to shout out [/r/learnprogramming](https://reddit.com/r/learnprogramming) on Reddit, which is an absolutely fantastic community.

# Learn skills that will get you a job

This one seems obvious, but it's very easy to focus on shiny new tech coming out of Silicon Valley instead of what's currently being used locally to you. For example: if all of the local developer jobs are asking for .NET, there's no point learning to build an API with [Phoenix](https://www.phoenixframework.org/). I understand that it's in our nature as developers to be attracted to shiny objects, but there's plenty of time to explore this stuff once you've already got a job.

_Side note about .NET jobs: they might not seem the most interesting places to work, but learning a strong enterprise language such as C# will open a lot of doors in your career, and there are always .NET jobs no matter where you live._
