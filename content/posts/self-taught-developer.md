_This post is a response to multiple people asking me how they could become a software developer without a degree, and I intend to come back and update this with any other wisdom I gather as I get further into my career._

# Introduction

If I were to ask somebody what qualifications I would need to get into software development, they would probably tell me I needed a degree in computer science or software engineering, and that I'd also need to be some kind of genius. I never went to university, and quite frankly I barely made it through my A-Levels thanks to a gripping addiction to World of Warcraft. I'm also not a genius by any stretch of the imagination, as my partner will often point out.

The tech industry is in the midst of a massive skills shortage, and this problem doesn't look like it's going away any time soon. Despite the number of people studying computer science going up every year, a lot of degrees are still failing to teach people the right skills to succeed in the industry after graduation. There's the [infamous blog post](https://blog.codinghorror.com/why-cant-programmers-program/) from Jeff Atwood that is still every bit as true despite being thirteen years old. Companies are desperate to hire developers, so they are looking at other avenues such as bootcamps or apprenticeships to build their workforce, and of course those of us that are self taught.

# Learning syntax is not learning to code

I've helped a number of people learn how to code, and something I see very often is that they will try to memorise the syntax of a language before they even try to write anything. Programming is more about solving problems than writing code, and the best way to get better at solving problems is to actually solve problems.

If you forget the syntax, or are unsure of a method name, __google it__. It's one of the worst kept secrets in the industry that even the best developers are googling on a daily basis. To solve a problem you must break it down into smaller problems, and from there you write code to solve those smaller problems. Google can tell you how to add an item to a list in Python, but it cannot tell you how your business logic should work.

# Don't just follow a tutorial

Websites like [Code Academy](https://codecademy.com) are absolutely great for getting you started with the very basics of coding, but there comes a point where you really need to build something of your own to progress. Anybody can follow a tutorial, but real growth happens when you start your own project, break it, and then figure out how you fix it. 

You're better off coming up with your own project, as you'd understand the problem better, but if you're really stuck for ideas then you could try the classic __to-do list__. Another suggestion would be to finish a tutorial project and then try adding additional features to it, such as adding __review__ functionality to your Movie List site.

# There's no shame in marketing

When it comes to applying for a job, you'll be up against computer science graduates, so you need to make sure your application stands out from the crowd. By this, I don't mean using a fancy font on your CV, or writing it in pig latin, but instead having a portfolio of work to show and conveying that you are a serious candidate. * __Put all of your code on Github:__ It doesn't matter how simple or boring your code is, get it on there. Employers just want to see that you actually know how to code, and having a few active repositories on your Github will do absolute wonders for your prospects.
