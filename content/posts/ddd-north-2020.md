I just got back from [DDD North](https://www.dddnorth.co.uk/): a free, one day conference in Hull that is attended by a few hundred .NET developers each year. It's my second time attending the conference, and I attended with a large group from [3Squared](https://www.3squared.com). The annual trip to DDD North is something of a staple in the dev team's calendar, and is always a great experience.

I like attending conferences because it allows me to see where the industry is going, and predict the skills I need to acquire to keep my skill set up to date; this one is a bonus because it didn't cost me a penny to attend! When I compared common themes from this year and last year's conference, I did notice a few trends that I thought would be interesting to write about further.

## Microservices were probably a mistake

Last year's conference was absolutely dominated by talk of microservices, continuous integration and containers; even in the hallways between talks. This year appeared to be dominated by talks about why you probably shouldn't be doing microservices, and how you should probably try building a decent monolith before you try your hand at distributed systems.

A lot of the people that were delivering these talks worked on projects that probably shouldn't have been using microservices in the first place, and the issues that they had encountered were symptomatic of this. My big take home was that microservices will almost always be the wrong decision if you aren't willing to commit to a huge culture shift.

## People are talking a lot more about Security

There was a lot more discussion about Application and Data Security at this year's conference, and these seemed to be some of the most popular talks of the day. It seems that a lot of organisations are starting to get serious about security, and techniques such as threat modelling are being used to 'harden' web applications.

## The DevOps experiment is succeeding

Resiliency and monitoring are typically more of an operational concern, and the fact that these talks were some of the most popular among developers shows that the DevOps philosophy is having some influence on the .NET world.


# What's Hot?

* Security
* Resiliency
* Serverless

# What's Not?

* Microservices
* Functional Programming 😢

# What is still a big deal?
